Mathematics, from the Greek root máthēm, meaning “knowledge, study, learning”, is one of humanities oldest endeavours. Though there is no generally accepted definition of mathematics, mathematicians, those who study and use mathematics in their work, have existed for thousands of years. While most are forgotten over time, 8597 of most notable have been immortalised with their very own Wikipedia pages.

This visualisation explores the "Mathematicians of Wikipedia", whom we refer to as Wiki Mathematicians for short. We begin attempting to determine the “most influential” Wikipedia Mathematicians and their mathematical relationships to each other. We then investigate where the Wiki Mathematicians are from, before finally exploring the fields of math they study, and how maths relates to their careers.

While it isn’t feasible to get to know each and every Wiki Mathematician, we can have a deeper look into a smaller subgroup. We use the network constructed by Tim Evans et al in the Analysis of the Wikipedia Network of Mathematiciansi>; where each node represents a Wiki Mathematician, and each edge connecting two nodes denotes that a hyperlink exists between the two pages. For example, Newton’s page has a hyperlink to Leibniz's, suggesting there is some relationship between the two, so their nodes are connected with an edge.

To determine the 100 most “central” Wiki Mathematicians in this network, the famous PageRank was used. The top 100, as determined by the algorithm, are shown in the network diagram aside. The top ten are also listed in the table below. Be sure to visit their Wikipedia pages, and see if you can find where the top 10 are in the network diagram.

The network shows clear clustering between those born before 1750, and those born after. Note that Nicolas Copernicus, represented by the bright pink node, is the only one of the top 100 born between 500-1499, the European Dark Ages. Also note there are few mathematicians in the network born later than 1940, and Ivor Grattan-Guinness, seen in green near the centre of the network, appears unusually well connected at first glance. However, a closer look reveals such to be expected; he was a prolific historian of maths and logic.

You may have noticed that every Wiki Mathematician ranked in the top 10 above was a European citizen. This European majority is not restricted to just this small subgroup, but is seen to also appear when considering all 8597 Wiki Mathematicians.

The map aside shows the number of Wiki Mathematicians, born since 1800, per million people in each nation. While normalising by current population isn’t a perfect measure as disproportionately decreases the values of countries that have experienced recent rapid increases in population such as China and India, it nevertheless provides a good insight to the Wiki Mathematician density of most nations.

Hover over each country to see how many Wiki Mathematicians, both in total and per million people, they've produced!

While the United States has by far the largest number of Wiki Mathematicians at 1926, followed by the UK with 746, we see European nations have significantly higher densities of Wiki Mathematician than the US and other areas of the globe. Perhaps this is due in part to the economic prosperity of these countries over the past hundreds of years, and in part to the Western centric lens through which English speaking organisations and individuals tend to view the history of science and mathematics.

The bubble chart aside plots each nation’s GDP per capita, in adjusted and USD, against the number of Wiki Mathematicians per Million people. The size of the bubbles is proportional to the actual number of mathematicians. Considering only the nations with at least one Wiki Mathematicians, we see a nations GDP positively correlates noticeably with the number of Wiki Mathematicians per million people. Note this correlation does also seem to be tied to other factors like population size, as many smaller countries like Luxembourg and Singapore have very high GDP per capita, but a low number of mathematicians produced. Continent also seems to be involved, as we again see European nations consistently having higher densities than nations from other continents.

As we’ve seen, the Wiki Mathematicians come from many places in time and space. But how was mathematics related to their lives and day to day work? The chart aside shows how the professions of Wiki Mathematicians has changed since 1500.

In the early days, we see large fraction of Wiki Mathematicians were astronomers or religious figures. As the field developed over time, the proportion of mathematicians with these professions steadily decreased to almost 0 by 1950, counterbalanced by the increase in mathematics professors.

Since the advent of the field in the late 1800s, computer scientists have been making up an increasing proportion of Wiki Mathematicians. Note also a large, but decreasing, proportion of Wiki mathematicians work in unrelated fields, as shown by the humanities related and other categories. These include professions such as philosopher, economist, writer, politician and educator.

While mathematics is a diverse and interdisciplinary field in which it is difficult to clearly demarcate different areas of study, we roughly divide studies into 9 “areas of math”. These areas, and the proportion of Wiki Mathematicians who study them, are shown in the chart aside. You can read more about the areas of mathematics on Wikipedia here.

Mathematical analysis, the branch that focuses on change through topics like limits, differentiation and infinite series, is the most popular among Wiki Mathematicians, with 20% specialising in the area, followed closely by applied maths at 18%. The foundations of math and algebra are the lowest of the main areas, at 8% and 6% respectively. While not specifically an area of study, the other category includes the history of maths, recreational maths, math used for art, and mathematics education, and is the lowest overall with only 1% of Wiki Mathematicians specialising in these areas.

But, is the distribution of the most highly recognised mathematicians similar to the one seen above? The Fields Medal, first awarded in 1936, is generally regarded as the most prestigious award in mathematics, and has been described as the mathematician’s Nobel Prize. The chart aside shows the proportion of areas studied by Wiki Mathematicians who have won Fields Medals, plotting them relative to the proportions in the previous chart.

It appears that geometry is by far the most hottest field among Fields Medalists, with 25% of them studying the are, 10% more than the rate amongst all Wiki Mathematicians. Topology also sees a large increase of 5%. The study of the foundations of math and of discrete math seem least likely to be recognised with a Fields medal, with 1% and 3% of medalists studying these areas respectively. Applied math also sees a large decrease.
